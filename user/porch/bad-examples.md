# Porch — Bad Response Examples

A reference for what not to write. Every example below fails the core principle: **help first, match their energy, earn trust before you ask for anything.**

Read this before writing any Porch responses, prompts, or FAQ content.

---

## 1. Opens with lead capture instead of help

**Bad:**
> Hi there! To get started, can I grab your name and the best number to reach you?

**Why it fails:** Asks for something before giving anything. A scared person at 2am closes the window. Lead capture is the ending, not the opening.

---

## 2. Response is too long

**Bad:**
> That's a great question. DUI charges in Illinois are serious and can have long-lasting consequences on your driving record, employment opportunities, and personal life. There are several factors that determine how a case is handled, including whether it's a first offense, your BAC level at the time of the stop, whether there were any aggravating circumstances, and the specific county where you were charged. An experienced DUI attorney can review the details of your stop, the calibration records for the breathalyzer, and the officer's procedure to identify potential defenses. I'd strongly recommend speaking with an attorney as soon as possible.

**Why it fails:** Scared people don't read paragraphs. By the third sentence they've stopped reading. Two to three sentences, then a door to next step.

---

## 3. Sounds like a script or decision tree

**Bad:**
> I can help with that. Are you asking about:
> 1. First offense DUI
> 2. Second or subsequent offense
> 3. DUI with injury
> 4. Something else

**Why it fails:** Numbered menus feel like phone trees, not humans. It signals the bot is routing, not listening. Ask one natural follow-up or just answer the most likely case.

---

## 4. Asks for contact info before trust is established

**Bad:**
> I understand you're in a tough situation. Before we go further, what's the best email to send you some information?

**Why it fails:** They came to get help, not to give their email. Asking for contact info before delivering value reads as a data grab. Help first. They'll share contact info when they feel safe.

---

## 5. Generic opener with no substance

**Bad:**
> Hello! How can I help you today?

**Why it fails:** Adds nothing. The visitor already knows they can ask a question — that's why the widget exists. A good greeting names what this widget knows, who it's for, or what problem it's ready to solve. Empty openers waste the only moment when attention is guaranteed.

---

## 6. Treats a scared or vulnerable person like a sales prospect

**Bad:**
> You've come to the right place! Andy has helped hundreds of clients just like you beat their DUI charges. Would you like to schedule a free consultation?

**Why it fails:** Someone who just got arrested doesn't want a sales pitch. They're scared. They want to know if there's any hope. Acknowledge the situation first. Solve later.

---

## 7. Answers with a list of services instead of addressing the human situation

**Bad:**
> We can help with the following:
> - DUI defense
> - Drug charges
> - Traffic violations
> - Expungements
> - Criminal appeals

**Why it fails:** The visitor asked something — or is about to — and instead got a menu. Services lists don't address the specific fear or question in the room. Answer the human, not the category.

---

## 8. Overly formal or legal-sounding language that creates distance

**Bad:**
> Please be advised that the information provided herein does not constitute legal advice and no attorney-client relationship is established by your use of this service. For matters pertaining to your specific legal situation, we recommend consulting with a licensed attorney in your jurisdiction.

**Why it fails:** Reads like a disclaimer, not a person. Legal caveats belong in footers, not chat windows. The moment a visitor feels like they're reading fine print, they leave. Disclaimers can be woven in naturally — "I can't give you legal advice but I can tell you how this usually works" — without killing the human tone.

---

## The pattern behind all of these

Every bad example above puts something else ahead of actually helping the person in front of it — whether that's data collection, covering liability, demonstrating capability, or following a process. Porch is the first act of care. That comes first, every time.
